A campus for living and learning.

The Memory Health Life Center is a 54-bed, 29-acre brain-health village rising in Quitman, Texas, adjacent to UT Health Quitman. This page walks through the mission, the site, the care model, the numbers, and the downloads behind it.

Our Mission

What we are here to do.

The Wood County Health Care Foundation began with one promise: build a place where families do not have to leave East Texas to find good dementia care, and where the people who provide that care can learn how to do it well.

The Site & Region

29 acres, next door to a hospital.

The Center for Memory Health & Education rises on 29 acres owned by the Wood County Central Hospital District, directly adjacent to UT Health's Quitman Hospital campus. The siting is intentional — a clinical neighbor a short walk away, and a regional position that puts millions of Texans within reach.

The site

Adjacent to UT Health Quitman.

  • Direct adjacency to UT Health's Quitman Hospital — clinical support a walk away

What's on the Campus

Six places, one campus.

Not a single building with corridors and doors. A village — residential dwellings, a clinic, a barn, gardens, reflection grounds, and student housing — designed so daily life feels like a neighborhood, not an institution.

Resident Homes

54 residential dwellings. Each is its own apartment-style home with open floor plan and large windows. No long, cold hallways lined with doors. Wall colors, paintings, and sculpture along common areas. Every aspect of design is tailored to those with memory issues.

Clinic, Education & Research

A medical clinic serving residents and the public, with educational opportunities for doctors, nurses, allied health professionals, and family caregivers. Research that helps other facilities across the nation adopt the same model.

Reflection Lake & Paths

A reflection lake with groomed walking paths, full of trees, green grass, and wildflowers. The healing power of outdoor spaces is the centerpiece of the campus, not an afterthought tucked behind the buildings.

Working Barn & Garden

Horses and tame animals housed in separate facilities residents can visit. A garden where residents can grow and enjoy purposeful, productive lives. The barn is a place to interact, not a backdrop.

Common Spaces

Dining facilities, entertainment areas, gathering spaces. The rhythms of a normal day — meals together, a movie, a porch in the afternoon — built back into the floor plan.

Student Housing

Dormitory housing for students, interns, residents, and SOM faculty participating in the living-laboratory experience. They learn how to better communicate with people facing memory challenges — and they help carry the day.

The Care Model

Hogeweyk-inspired. Zeisel-trained.

The care model was pioneered by John Zeisel, PhD — a leading expert on dementia care and a former Harvard professor — and built on five foundational principles. The same five principles shape every program, every space, and every interaction on the campus.

  1. 1

    Community Design

    Spaces designed to create a sense of warmth, belonging, and security — not the sterile geometry of institutional care.

  2. 2

    Communication

    Staff and family training in the language and presence dementia care requires — how to listen, how to respond, how to stay.

  3. 3

    Engagement

    Meaningful daily activity. Purpose, not distraction. A reason to wake up and a place to go.

  4. 4

    Creative Arts

    Music, painting, theater, and movement as therapeutic practice — pathways the disease cannot close.

  5. 5

    Nature

    Outdoor air, light, animals, water, planting. Built into the campus, not added on later.

The foundation reviewed dementia-village programs in the United States and in Sweden, Norway, Ireland, and England before settling on this combination — a hybrid that brings the Hogeweyk lineage to East Texas and adds an education and research wing the European models do not have.
